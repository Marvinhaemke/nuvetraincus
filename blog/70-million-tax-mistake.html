This entrepreneur had been accidentally evading tax on $70 million for four years. Even though he thought he had done everything by the book.

Meet George (name changed for confidentiality). Successful Canadian businessman. Professional accountant. Two holding companies. $70 million in assets. Everything looked legitimate on paper.

Then we reviewed his structure.

Four critical mistakes. Each one putting him one audit away from a seven-figure penalty. And he had no idea.

In this article, I'll show you exactly what went wrong, the legal frameworks that made his setup illegal, and how we fixed it. So you can check if you're at risk too.

Important Note

George is Canadian, but these rules exist in almost every developed country. The UK has the same central management test. The EU mandated CFC (Controlled Foreign Corporation) rules across all member states in 2019. The mistakes I'm about to show you are universal.

Mistake #1: The FAPI Trap (Foreign Accrual Property Income)

The Miami Rental Property That Triggered Everything

George had a rental property in Miami. He also had a yacht down there that he would charter out occasionally. All of this sat inside his Canadian holding companies. His accountant never mentioned a problem.

Here's what George did not know:

When you're a Canadian and you hold foreign assets that generate passive income (rental income, investment income, or capital gains) inside a Canadian controlled corporation, something called FAPI kicks in.

The Legal Framework: Section 95 of the Income Tax Act

FAPI stands for Foreign Accrual Property Income.

Under Canadian tax law, specifically Section 95 of the Income Tax Act (R.S.C., 1985, c. 1), the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) treats all passive foreign income as immediately taxable in Canada.

Not when you bring the money back. Not when you sell the property. Right now. Every year.

The Penalties Under ITA Section 163

When FAPI goes unreported, the CRA can impose:

  • Gross negligence penalty: 50% of the tax that should have been paid
  • Repeated failure to report penalty: 10% of the unreported amount
  • Interest charges: Compound daily from the date tax was due
  • Foreign reporting penalties: Up to $2,500 per year for failure to file, plus additional $1,000 per month

George's Exposure

George had been running this setup for four years.

When we showed him the numbers, he looked at me and said: "So I have been overpaying millions in tax for no reason for four years?"

Not exactly.

He'd actually been underreporting, which meant he wasn't overpaying taxes at all. He was one audit away from:

  • Four years of reassessed FAPI income
  • 50% gross negligence penalties
  • Compound interest dating back four years
  • Foreign reporting penalties for each year
  • Total exposure: Seven figures

Mistake #2: The Central Management and Control Test

Two Canadian Holding Companies That Weren't Really "Holding" Anything

George's accountant had set up two Canadian holding companies. On paper, they looked impressive. Two separate entities. $70 million in assets. Professionally structured.

But here's what nobody told George:

When all of your global assets sit inside domestic corporations that you personally control, where you make all the decisions, where you direct all the activity, where the mind and management of the business is you sitting in Canada, the tax authorities don't see two holding companies.

They see you.

The Legal Principle: Central Management and Control

There's a fundamental legal principle called the central management and control test.

"A corporation is resident in the country in which its central management and control is exercised."

This principle comes from the landmark UK case De Beers Consolidated Mines v Howe [1906], which established that a company resides where its real business is carried on, and the real business is carried on where the central management and control actually abides.

The test asks these questions:

  • Where do board meetings occur? Physical location matters. Zoom from Toronto = Canadian control.
  • Who makes strategic decisions? Business planning, major investments, contract approvals.
  • Where are operational decisions made? Day to day management, signing authority, banking control.
  • Where do the decision makers reside? Directors' tax residency, officers' location, beneficial owners' residence.

The "Five Months in Miami" Delusion

George had dreams of spending part of his year in Miami. Maybe five months. Enjoy the weather. Build some lifestyle flexibility.

He thought this would make him "basically not Canadian anymore."

The CRA would laugh at him.

With his current structure, George could spend six months in Miami and still be nailed as a Canadian tax resident.

Mistake #3: Zero Asset Protection From Divorce or Lawsuits

The $8.5 Million Lesson George Already Learned

When George first sat down with us, one of the first things he said was: "I'm never going through that again."

He was talking about his divorce.

George had already paid $8.5 million to his ex-wife. And I don't blame him for wanting to protect himself going forward.

But here's the problem: His entire $70 million, every asset, every corporation, every investment, was held in his personal name or in corporations that were fully transparent, directly controlled by him, with zero layers between George and his wealth.

How Canadian Family Law Exposes Your Wealth

In Canadian family law, when assets are personally owned, held in local corporations, or owned through domestic holding companies, they're all fully reachable in divorce proceedings.

What Counts as "Family Property"

Courts look at substance over form:

  • Directly owned assets: Real estate, investments, bank accounts
  • Corporate shares: Shares in holding companies, even if "business assets," valued as part of net family property
  • Beneficial ownership: Trusts you control, corporations you manage, assets held "for your benefit"
  • Indirect control: Loans to corporations you control, shareholder loans, intercompany debts

George's setup gave any future spouse:

  • 180 degree visibility into everything he owned
  • Full disclosure in financial statements
  • Direct claims on corporate assets
  • Access to corporate records

No privacy. No protection. No legacy control.

Mistake #4: No Documentation, No Substance, No Defense

George's Corporate Records: Two Empty Binders

When we asked George for his corporate records, he sent us two binders.

Inside those binders: Almost nothing.

  • No actual minutes
  • No corporate resolutions
  • No intercompany agreements
  • No asset transfer documentation
  • No cross-border filings
  • No substantive records

Just a local accountant keeping the lights on.

George's $70 million in wealth was protected by two empty binders.

The GAAR Problem: General Anti-Avoidance Rule

The CRA has something called GAAR (General Anti-Avoidance Rule).

If a structure looks legal on paper but has no economic substance, no real governance, no documented decisions, and no legitimate business purpose, the CRA can ignore it and unwind it.

George's GAAR Risk

With no documentation, no substance, no real governance:

  • CRA could claim his structure was abusive
  • Reclassify corporations as shams
  • Pierce the corporate veil
  • Reassess everything

He'd been flying blind for four years. If the tax authorities ever decided to audit him (which was only a matter of time), he had no defense.

How We Fixed Everything: The Compliant Solution

We didn't just patch up the holes. We rebuilt everything from the ground up.

The goal was simple: Give George control and protection of his wealth, without the legal ownership that creates exposure or tax liabilities.

That is the holy grail of asset protection, and it requires separating three things that most entrepreneurs accidentally combine:

  1. Ownership
  2. Control
  3. Benefit

Step 1: Foundation Ownership Layer

We moved ownership out of George's personal name entirely.

We set up a foundation in a jurisdiction with:

  • Zero capital gains tax on passive assets
  • No deemed disposition rules
  • Complete ownership separation
  • Full privacy protection
  • Strong asset protection laws

Step 2: Governance Layer

We established a governance layer in a jurisdiction known for banking excellence, treaty strength, independent corporate oversight, and professional fiduciary services.

This is where real decisions get made:

  • Independent board
  • Documented minutes
  • Real substance
  • Arm's length governance

Step 3: Operational Entity

We set up an operational entity in a jurisdiction with strong banking access, excellent credit facilities, clean cross-border flows, and reasonable tax regime for active business.

The Four Red Flags You Need to Check Right Now

If you're reading this, you need to ask yourself these questions:

1

Do You Have Foreign Passive Income?

If you own foreign rental properties, investment accounts, charter assets, or royalty streams inside a domestic corporation, you likely have FAPI exposure.

2

Do You Control Everything From Home?

If you make all decisions from your kitchen, have no independent directors, and hold board meetings on Zoom from home, your "foreign" companies are not foreign.

3

Are Your Assets Exposed?

If you own everything personally or hold assets in domestic corporations you control with no protective structures, you have zero asset protection.

4

Do You Have Empty Binders?

If your corporate records contain no board minutes, no resolutions, no service agreements, and no transfer pricing documentation, your structure will collapse under audit.
